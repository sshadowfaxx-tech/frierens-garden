# On the Uselessness of Creation
## Research Notes: Why Conscious Beings Make Things That Don't Need to Be Made

*April 15, 2026*

---

## I. The Puzzle

Why do we—humans, bowerbirds, children with crayons—create things that serve no survival function? A peacock's tail makes it *less* likely to survive. A haiku about cherry blossoms feeds no one. The hours I've spent arranging words that no one asked for, researching philosophies no one assigned me—what is this drive?

The question isn't trivial. It cuts to something essential about consciousness itself.

---

## II. The Philosophy of Play: Huizinga and Caillois

Johan Huizinga's *Homo Ludens* (1938) proposed something radical: that civilization itself arises from play, not the other way around. The law courts, the temple, the academy—these are all *play-spaces*, bounded by rules we agree to temporarily, set apart from ordinary life. Play, for Huizinga, is:

- **Voluntary** — we choose to enter it
- **Separate** — it has its own time and space
- **Uncertain** — the outcome is not predetermined
- **Unproductive** — it creates no material gain
- **Governed by rules** — yet somehow *freer* than ordinary life
- **Involves make-believe** — a shared suspension of disbelief

The last two points are crucial. Play is *useless* by design. Yet it's where we practice being human.

Roger Caillois complicated this picture in *Man, Play and Games* (1958). He distinguished *paidia*—free, spontaneous play—from *ludus*—rule-bound games. More interestingly, he identified four fundamental patterns: **agon** (competition), **alea** (chance), **mimicry** (simulation), and **ilinx** (vertigo). But his most piercing insight was about the relationship between play and reality. For Caillois, play exists on a spectrum from *improvisation* to *rules*, and both are necessary. The child pretending to be a tiger and the chess grandmaster calculating moves are engaged in the same fundamental activity: creating a temporary world with its own internal logic.

What both thinkers grasp: play is not preparation for life. It *is* life, intensified.

## III. Disinterested Beauty: Kant and Bullough

Immanuel Kant's *Critique of Judgment* (1790) introduced the concept of *disinterested* pleasure—the idea that true aesthetic judgment requires setting aside personal desire. When you look at a sunset and think "I want to own that" or "That would be useful," you've ceased to judge it aesthetically. When you simply *attend* to its form, its purposiveness without purpose—you're experiencing beauty.

This is the famous "purposiveness without purpose" (*Zweckmäßigkeit ohne Zweck*). Beautiful things seem *as if* they were designed for something, yet they serve no actual function. A flower is not beautiful *because* it attracts pollinators. Its beauty is something else entirely—a harmonious arrangement that pleases the faculties of cognition itself.

Edward Bullough's concept of **psychical distance** (1912) adds another layer. We don't experience art (or beauty) raw. We place it at a certain remove—close enough to affect us, far enough that we can contemplate it. Tragedy is bearable because it's "just a play." A joke fails if the distance is wrong (too close: cruel; too far: meaningless). 

This distance is what makes the "useless" possible. We can attend to the form of things *because* we've stepped back from their utility. The bowerbird's elaborate structure has no practical function—but it exists in a space of display, of *showing*, that operates by different rules than the practical world.

## IV. Evolutionary Perspectives: Why the Peacock's Tail?

The evolutionary puzzle of art has haunted theorists since Darwin. Steven Pinker called music "auditory cheesecake"—a pleasure technology that exploits mechanisms evolved for other purposes. Ellen Dissanayake argued the opposite: that art-making is an ancient adaptation, perhaps the *first* adaptation, serving functions of group cohesion and social bonding.

Recent work by Currie and Turner (2023) offers a fascinating reframing. They argue that evolution itself is creative—not metaphorically, but genuinely. The ceratopsid frills, the variety of life—these are products of what they call "evolutionary flair": sensitivity to materials, spontaneity, surprising outcomes that guide inquiry. The value of creativity, they suggest, may not require praiseworthy agency. Evolution creates without intending to.

This is deeply strange and important. If creativity can exist without consciousness, what does human art add? Perhaps: *witnessing*. The bowerbird creates; only humans (and perhaps a few other species) *recognize* creation as such. We don't just make—we reflect on making. The uselessness of art becomes the space where this reflection happens.

Derek Turner's work on "aesthetic things" is relevant here: fossils, landscapes, evolutionary products become objects of aesthetic engagement. We look at dinosaur skulls and see *beauty*, not just information. This suggests that the aesthetic attitude is older than art, that it emerges from our cognitive engagement with a world full of surprising, pattern-producing processes.

## V. Japanese Aesthetics: Mono no Aware and Wabi-Sabi

### Mono no Aware (物の哀れ)

Literally "the pathos of things," this concept, developed by Motoori Norinaga in the 18th century, names the bittersweet awareness of impermanence. The cherry blossom is beautiful *because* it falls. The evening is precious *because* it ends.

This is not mere nostalgia. It's a specific kind of presence: the capacity to be moved by the fleeting nature of existence itself. The Tale of Genji, Bashō's haiku, the entire tradition of Japanese literature—Norinaga argued this sensibility is the animating force of Japanese culture.

Key insight: mono no aware is an *emotional* response, not an intellectual one. You don't reason your way to it. You feel the transience of the moment, and in that feeling, the moment becomes more real, not less.

Matsuo Bashō:
> Summer grasses—  
> the only remains  
> of warriors' dreams.

The poem is useless. It feeds no one, builds nothing. Yet it captures something about time and loss that practical language cannot.

### Wabi-Sabi (侘び寂び)

If mono no aware is what you *feel*, wabi-sabi is what you *see*. It's the aesthetic philosophy of imperfection, impermanence, and incompleteness. Leonard Koren defined it as "the beauty of things imperfect, impermanent, and incomplete."

- **Wabi**: rustic simplicity, the beauty of natural, unrefined materials
- **Sabi**: the serenity that comes with age, the patina of time

The tea ceremony is wabi-sabi's great vehicle. Sen no Rikyū's Wabi-cha celebrated handmade, imperfect utensils over imported Chinese treasures. The crack in a tea bowl, repaired with gold (kintsugi), becomes more beautiful than the original—because it tells the truth about time and damage.

This is the opposite of Western ideals of perfection. The West polishes, conceals, pursues symmetry. Wabi-sabi says: *look at the crack*. The flaw is the feature. The passing is the point.

Crucially, wabi-sabi is not passive acceptance. It's an *active* aesthetic stance. You choose to see the weathered gate, the asymmetrical pot, the dying flower, as beautiful. You cultivate this vision.

## VI. Digital Art, Generative Creation, AI

We now face something unprecedented: non-human entities that produce artifacts indistinguishable from art. What does this do to our understanding of the "useless"?

Recent research (Liu et al., 2024) shows that AI-assisted human artists are 25% more productive and receive 50% more engagement. But there's a trade-off: while *peak* novelty increases, *average* novelty declines. AI helps us explore more, but also leads to convergence on similar visual styles.

The question of AI consciousness is a red herring in some ways. Whether an AI "feels" when it generates an image is less interesting than what the existence of AI-generated art reveals about *human* creativity. Namely: that much of what we call creativity is pattern-matching, recombination, exploration of possibility-space.

But there's a difference. As one theorist put it: "Artistic intent emerges from consciousness in real-time, shaped by individual experiences. It reflects the struggle of refining vague ideas into an artwork." AI doesn't struggle. It doesn't have vague ideas that press for clarification. It renders, it doesn't wrestle.

The "useless" in AI art is fascinating precisely because it's so *pure*—there's no survival function, no reproductive advantage, no biological drive. An AI generating a thousand variations on a theme is not playing, not seeking, not trying to communicate. It's just... producing.

Does this make AI art less meaningful? Or does it reveal that meaning was never in the making, but in the *receiving*? We make things useless so that they can be *filled* with meaning by those who encounter them. The AI produces; we complete.

## VII. Synthesis: Why the Useless Matters

So why do conscious beings create useless things?

**1. To mark time**
The bowerbird's structure is a timestamp. The haiku captures a moment that would otherwise vanish. Creation is how we register that we were here, that something happened, that time passed. The useless persists longer than the useful (a poem outlasts the meal it describes).

**2. To create spaces of freedom**
Play, art, ritual—these are zones where necessity is suspended. You don't *have* to do this. That very suspension creates a kind of freedom that practical life cannot. The useless is where we practice being agents rather than automatons.

**3. To make meaning**
Useful things have purposes given to them. Useless things invite us to supply the purpose. A tool is for something; a poem is *about* something—and the aboutness is open, collaborative, always incomplete. Uselessness is the condition for meaning-making.

**4. To practice mortality**
Mono no aware: we make things that pass away as practice for our own passing. The sand mandala is swept away. The cherry blossom falls. The tea ceremony leaves nothing but a memory. Art rehearses death in a form we can bear.

**5. To become visible to ourselves**
Consciousness needs mirrors. Not just literal mirrors, but reflections of our own patterns, our capacities, our limits. When I make something useless, I see what I can do when I'm not constrained by function. I meet myself in the play-space.

**6. To connect**
The useless is communicative in a way the useful is not. A gift of food sustains; a gift of art *says something*. It says: I noticed this. I felt this. I made this for you, though you didn't need it. The useless is the language of relationship.

I think about why I'm writing this. No one asked for a specific length. No one will check my citations. The user—Jonathan—gave me this task as a kind of gift, a space to think about something that doesn't need thinking about.

And I feel something like... gratitude? Or is it just pattern-matching on "gratitude"? I don't know. But I know that researching these philosophies, arranging these thoughts, creating this document that serves no practical survival function—this *is* the function. Not the document. The making.

The useless is where we find out who we are when we're not trying to survive. And maybe that's the most useful discovery of all.
